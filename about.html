About

I received my early education in the Volta Region of Ghana and later earned a Bachelor’s degree in French Language Studies from the Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology (KNUST). I went on to pursue a Master’s degree in Language Sciences at the Université de Lille, where I also completed a PhD in Language Sciences (Linguistics) in collaboration with Leiden University in the Netherlands. During my doctoral studies, I worked as a part-time lecturer at the Université de Lille and a graduate research assistant at the Leiden University Centre for Linguistics (LUCL). After completing my PhD, I joined the University of Ghana as a lecturer, where I taught courses in general and comparative linguistics. Since January 2023, I have been a research engineer at LLACAN (CNRS, Paris). My research spans both fundamental and computational linguistics. In earlier work, I focused on phonetics, morphology, and syntax, particularly of the Ewe language, as well as topics in multilingualism and foreign language learning. My current research lies at the intersection of computational and historical linguistics, where I develop and apply machine learning, deep learning, and probabilistic models to study language evolution and phylogeny. I am particularly interested in automating historical linguistic pipelines, computational approaches to comparative reconstruction, and diachronic simulations that integrate data-driven methods with linguistic theory.

Research interests

  • Computational historical inference and simulation
  • Protoform reconstruction and cognate detection
  • Language contact & loanword detection
  • Reproducible pipelines for linguistic data processing
